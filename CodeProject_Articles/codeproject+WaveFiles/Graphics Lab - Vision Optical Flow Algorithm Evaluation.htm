Optical Flow

Optical FlowAlgorithm Evaluation

Optical flow is the velocity field which warpsone image into another (usually very similar) image. This is illustratedby the images below. The sphere is rotating from left to right, generatingthe optical flow field shown in the center.

Image #1 Optical Flow Field Image #2

Almost 20 years ago Horn & Schunck [1] publishedthe seminal paper on optical flow calculation.  This paper sparkeda substantial research effort, producing an abundence of techniques forcalculating optical flow. Unfortunately because of the difficulties involvedin generating ground truth results for real image sequences, it has beenalmost impossible to evaluate our progress. Are the new algorithms reallyany better than Horn & Schunk's technique, published almost 20 yearsago?

While there have been previous attempts at evaluatingthe performance of optical flow techniques, they have usually used simplesequences generated by applying a distortion to a base image, since thecorresponding motion field is trivial to compute. Unfortunately, it isdifficult to know how relevant these results are to real  3D imagery,with all its associated complexities (for example motion discontinuities,complex 3D surfaces, camera noise, specular highlights, shadows, atmospherics,transparency).

In our evaluation we have used two methods togenerate more complex sequences with ground-truth data: a ray-tracerwhichgenerates optical flow, and a Tcl/Tk toolwhich allows us to generate ground truth optical flow from simple (ie polygonal)real sequences with a little help from the user.  Both of these toolsare available if you want to generate your own sequences (click on thelinks above), or you can download some existing sequences - just clickon one of the images below:

Many of the sequences above are small clips frommuch longer sequences. If you want the full sequence contact us and we may be able to ftp you the file, or send you a CDROM.

The optical flow maps are stored in our own PCMformat.

The top three synthetic sequences were used inan earlier study [1] to compare the performance of 8 optical flow algorithms.The results were a little surprising - Horn and Schunck [2] was one ofthe best performers. The other stand-out performer was the algorithm byProesmans et al [3].You can downloadour implementations of Proesmans's and Camus's [4] algorithms (C++ source,1.2k). Here is azipped version.

We have extended this study [5], using some real sequences (including the onesabove) and a few more synthetic sequences. The filestarred orzippedcontain 9 frames of the blocks sequence along with the ground truth flow fieldand the flow field produced by Proesman's algorithm.If you use any of this data in your research, please cite our paper [5].
