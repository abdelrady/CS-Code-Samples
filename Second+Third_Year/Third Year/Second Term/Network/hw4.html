Chapter 4 Problems

  1. Chapter 4 Problem 1.

    Consider some of the pros and cons of virtual-circuit and datagram networks.

    1. Suppose that in the network layer, routers were subject to stressful conditions that might cause them to fail fairly often. At a high level, what actions would need to be taken on such router failure? Does this argue in favor of VC or datagram architecture?
    2. Suppose that in order to provide a guarantee regarding the level of performance (for example, delay) that would be seen along a source-to-destination path, the network requires a sender to declare its peak traffic rate. If the declared peak traffic rate and the existing declared traffic rates are such that there is no way to get traffic from source to the destination that meets the required delay requirements, the source is not allowed access to the network. Would such an approach be more easily accomplished within a VC or a datagram architecture?

  2. Chapter 4 Problem 2.

    Consider a virtual-circuit network. Suppose the VC number is a 16-bit field.

    1. What is the maximum number of virtual circuits that can be carried over the link?
    2. Suppose a central node determines paths and VC numbers at connection setup. Suppose the same VC number is used on each link along the VC's path. Describe how the central node might determine the VC number at connection setup. Is it possible that there are fewer VCs in progress than the maximum in (a) yet there is no common free VC number?
    3. Suppose that different VC numbers are permitted in each link along a VC's path. During connection setup, after end-to-end path is determined, describe how the links can choose their VC numbers and configure their forwarding tables in decentralized manner, without reliance on a central node.
